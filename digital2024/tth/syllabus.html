Syllabus

ART 1800
Tuesday & Thursday
8am – 10:30am
KH B4009

Instructor: Leona Cheung

This course is an introduction to the practical and creative uses of digital tools for art and design. The course is structured for a fundamental understanding of digital art and technology in three core components: Pixel, Vector, and Time.

Each unit provides technical knowledge for the most common tools in the field, including standard practices in resolution, formatting, and file management. Pixel introduces raster image-making in Photoshop. Vector focuses on creating geometry-based drawings in Illustrator in respect to basic principles of art and design. Lastly, Time explores the time-based properties of motion graphics using After Effects, including how time can be (de)constructed or emphasized.

Projects

Student Presentation
Present an artist or designer with a 15-minute slide presentation.

P1: Event Flyer
Design a flyer to promote and celebrate an imaginary event using Photoshop. Understand how to create raster-based images and become familiar with raster-based software. Practice applying principles of composition, as well as integrating text and image to communicate a message or theme.

P2: Object Logo
Design a logo based on an object in Illustrator. With a focus on process, create vector-based drawings that are representational of an everyday object. Using various techniques, explore how to visually translate something physical from real life into the digital realm.

Micro-Project: Title Sequence
Create a short motion graphic for a book or music festival using After Effects.

P4: Self-Learning
Choose a piece of digital work (vector, raster, or time-based) that you find inspirational and would like to reverse-engineer. Identify key skills and techniques used in the creation of the piece that would be beneficial to add to your skillset. Research on your own how to acquire the skills you identified using available resources. Then recreate the relevant parts (or all) of the selected piece using your own imagery or style.
